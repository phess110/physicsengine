Capacitance

When a voltage \(V\) is applied across a capacitor, it induces a charge \(q\) on the plates of the capacitor. It turns out these are proportional, i.e $$q = CV,$$ where \(C\) is a constant called the capacitance of the capacitor. Find \(\vec{E}\) between the capacitors plates (typically, using Gauss' law). Compute \(V = -\int_i^f \vec{E}\cdot \mathrm{d}\vec{s}\) along a path following the direction of \(-\vec{E}\), i.e. from the negative plate to the positive plate. Since \(V = \frac{q}{C}\), we have $$C = \frac{q}{\int_{-}^{+} E\mathrm{d}s}.$$ Consider a parallel plate capacitor (\(A\) = plate area, \(d\) = plate separation). By Gauss' law, \(E = \frac{q}{\epsilon_0 A}.\) Thus \(V = \frac{qd}{\epsilon_0 A}.\) Finally, \(C = \frac{\epsilon_0 A }{d}.\) Consider a cylindrical capacitor (\(L\) = length, \(a\) = inner radius, \(b\) = outer radius). By Gauss' law, \(E = \frac{q}{2\pi\epsilon_0 rL},\) so \(V = \frac{q}{2\pi\epsilon_0 L} \ln\left(\frac{a}{b}\right).\) Thus \(C = \frac{2\pi\epsilon_0 L}{\ln\left(\frac{a}{b}\right) }.\)

Capacitors in Circuits

Capacitors in series will each have the same charge \(q\). The equivalent capacitor should have voltage drop \(V_{\mathrm{eq}} = V_1 +\ldots + V_n = \frac{q}{C_1} + \ldots + \frac{q}{C_n}\). On the other hand, \(V_{\mathrm{eq}} = \frac{q}{C_{\mathrm{eq}}},\) whence $$\frac{1}{C_{\mathrm{eq}}} = \frac{1}{C_1} + \ldots + \frac{1}{C_n}.$$ For capacitors in parallel, the voltage drop across each capacitor is the same, the equivalent capacitor should have charge \(q_{\mathrm{eq}} = q_1+\ldots + q_n\), hence $$C_{\mathrm{eq}} = C_1+\ldots + C_{n}.$$

Potential energy of a capacitor

\(\mathrm{d}W = -V \mathrm{d}q' =\frac{q'\mathrm{d}q'}{C}\) thus $$W = \frac{-1}{C} \int_0^q q' \mathrm{d}q' = \frac{-q^2}{2C}.$$ Hence $$U = \frac{q^2}{2C} = \frac{1}{2} CV^2.$$

Energy density of an electric field

For a parallel plate capacitor, \(C = \frac{\epsilon_0 A}{d}\). Subsituting \(Ed = V\) and \(\text{vol} = Ad\). $$u = \frac{U}{\text{vol}} = \frac{1}{2}\epsilon_0 E^2.$$

Dielectrics

\(\vec{E_0}\) between the plates induces a torque on electric dipoles within the material between the plates, causing the electric dipole moments to tend to align with \(\vec{E_0}\). The alignment of the dipole moments creates a field \(\vec{E'}\) opposite the direction of the applied field. The resulting field \(\vec{E} = \vec{E_0} - \vec{E'}\) has the same direction as \(\vec{E_0}\) but smaller magnitude. The dielectric constant of a material is defined by $$E = \frac{E_0}{\kappa}.$$For a vaccuum, \(\kappa = 1\), for all other materials \(\kappa > 1\). Let \(q\) be the initial charge on the plates when a vaccuum is present between the plates. Suppose we insert a dielectric with constant \(\kappa\). The alignment of the dipoles causes a small negative charge accumulation \(-q'\) on the surface of the dielectric near the positive plate (likewise, a small positive charge accumulation \(q'\) on the surface of the dielectric near the negative plate). By Gauss' Law applied to a Gaussian surface enclosing the plate and part of the dielectric, we have $$\epsilon_0 \oint \vec{E} \cdot \mathrm{d}\vec{A} = q-q',$$so \(E = \frac{q-q'}{\epsilon_0 A}\). On the other hand, for a parallel plate capacitor, \(E = \frac{E_0}{\kappa} = \frac{q}{\kappa \epsilon_0 A}\).
