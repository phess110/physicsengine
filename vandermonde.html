Vandermonde Matrices

Given real numbers, \(r_1,\ldots,r_{n}\), we define $$V_n = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & r_1 & r_1^2 & \ldots & r_1^{n-1}\\ 1 & r_2 & r_2^2 & \ldots & r_2^{n-1}\\ \vdots & \ddots & \ddots & \ddots & \vdots\\ 1 & r_n & r_n^2 & \ldots & r_n^{n-1} \end{bmatrix}.$$ We have that $$\det(V_n) = \prod_{1\leq i \lt j \leq n} (x_j-x_i).$$ We'll prove this formula by induction. The base case is easy. So now consider, $$V_{n+1} = \left[\begin{array}{cccc|c} & & & & r_1^{n}\\ & V_n & & & \vdots\\ & & & & r_n^{n} \\ \hline 1 & x & x^2 & \ldots & x^{n} \end{array}\right]$$ Take the determinant across the bottom row.

  • Then \(p(x) = \det(V_{n+1})\) is a polynomial of degree at most \(n\).
  • Note \(p(x_1) = \ldots = p(x_n) = 0\), thus \((x-r_i)\ |\ p(x)\) for \(1\leq i \leq n\). Hence, by degree considerations, $$p(x) = C \prod_{i=1}^n (x-r_i)$$where \(C\) is some constant.
  • Moreover, it is clear that the coefficient of \([x^n] = C = \det V_n\). Thus, by the inductive hypothesis, if we take \(x = r_{n+1}\), we have $$\det V_{n+1} = \prod_{1\leq i \lt j \leq n+1} (x_j-x_i).$$
