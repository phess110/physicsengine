Current

By convention the current is always drawn in the direction positive charge carriers would move. The current density \(\vec{J}\) is defined so that \(i = \int \vec{J} \cdot \mathrm{d}\vec{A}.\) \(\vec{J}\) has the same direction as \(i\). We can compute the drift speed \(v_d\) of electrons in a conductor. The amount of charge in a conductor of uniform cross section \(A\) and length \(L\) is \(q=(nAL)e\) where \(n\) is the number of charge carriers per unit volume. The time for an electron to travel across the conductor is \(t = \frac{L}{v_d}.\) Since \(i = \frac{q}{t} = \frac{nALev_d}{L}\) it follows \(v_d = \frac{i}{nAe} = \frac{J}{ne}\)$$\vec{v_d} = \frac{\vec{J}}{ne}.$$

Resistance & Resistivity

The resistance of an object is defined by \(R = \frac{V}{i}.\) The resistivity of a material is defined by \(\rho = \frac{E}{J}.\) The conductivity \(\sigma = \frac{1}{\rho}.\) For homogenous, isotropic conductors with uniform cross section, \(E = \frac{V}{L}\) and \(J = \frac{i}{A}.\) Thus $$\rho = \frac{E}{J} = \frac{VA}{iL} = R \frac{L}{A}.$$ Ohm's law is the assertion that \(i\) is always directly proportional to the applied potential. \(a = \frac{F}{m} = \frac{eE}{m}\) and \(v_d = a \tau = \frac{eE\tau}{m}\) where \(\tau\) is the average time between collision of the electron with atoms in the metal. Combining with \(v_d = \frac{J}{ne}\), we have \(E = \left(\frac{m}{e^2 n\tau}\right)J\). Since \(E =\rho J\), it follows \(\rho = \frac{m}{e^2n \tau}.\) Thus Ohm's law is the claim that \(\rho\) is constant as \(V\) varies, which is essentially a claim that \(\tau\) is constant. However, \(v_d\) is typically several orders of magnitude (say 10) smaller than its effective velocity due to its thermal energy; hence Ohm's law ostensibly holds (at least when temperature is held constant).

Power

Power is the rate of energy transfer. \(\mathrm{d}U = V\mathrm{d}q = Vi \mathrm{d}t \) Thus $$P = \frac{\mathrm{d}U}{\mathrm{d}t} = iV.$$ In particular, for a resistor $$P = i^2R = \frac{V^2}{R}.$$
